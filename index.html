Confidence Interval on the Mean
(Population Variance Unknown)
Based on Montgomery & Runger, Section 8.2

Topic: Estimating a Confidence Interval for a Population Mean (μ) when the Population Variance (σ²) is Unknown

  • Understand when to use the t-distribution.
  • Learn how to calculate the confidence interval for μ.
  • Interpret the results and their practical significance.

The Statistical Scenario

Assumptions:

A random sample of data, $X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n$, is taken from a Normal Distribution with:

  • Unknown Mean: μ
  • Unknown Variance: σ²

Objective: To construct a confidence interval for the true population mean, μ.

If σ² is known → Use the Z-distribution.
If σ² is unknown → Use the t-distribution.

From Z-Statistic to T-Statistic

When the population variance σ² is known, we use the Z-statistic:

$Z = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}} \sim N(0,1)$

When σ² is unknown, we replace it with the sample standard deviation, S. This introduces more uncertainty, leading to the T-statistic:

$T = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu}{S/\sqrt{n}} \sim t_{n-1}$

S: The Sample Standard Deviation (an estimate of σ).
n-1: The Degrees of Freedom (df).

Rationale: The t-distribution accounts for the additional variability introduced by estimating σ with S.

Comparing t-Distribution and Normal (Z) Distribution

  • Both are symmetric and centered at 0.
  • The t-distribution has "heavier tails," meaning a higher probability of extreme values.
  • As degrees of freedom ($n-1$) increase, the t-distribution approaches the Z-distribution.

Constructing the Confidence Interval

We start with the probability statement for the t-statistic:

$P\left(-t_{\alpha/2, n-1} \le \frac{\bar{X} - \mu}{S/\sqrt{n}} \le t_{\alpha/2, n-1}\right) = 1 - \alpha$

By rearranging the inequality to isolate μ, we derive the formula for the confidence interval:

$\bar{X} - t_{\alpha/2, n-1}\frac{S}{\sqrt{n}} \le \mu \le \bar{X} + t_{\alpha/2, n-1}\frac{S}{\sqrt{n}}$

Where $t_{\alpha/2, n-1}$ is the critical value from the t-distribution for a given confidence level ($1-\alpha$) and degrees of freedom ($n-1$).

Example 8.5: Alloy Adhesion

Problem: A tensile adhesion test was performed on U-700 alloy specimens. Find a 95% confidence interval for the true mean load at failure (μ).

Sample Data:

  • Sample Size (n) = 22
  • Sample Mean ($\bar{x}$) = 13.71 MPa
  • Sample Standard Deviation (s) = 3.55 MPa

Calculation:

Degrees of Freedom (df) = $n - 1 = 21$.
For 95% confidence, $\alpha = 0.05$, so $t_{\alpha/2, n-1} = t_{0.025, 21} = 2.080$.

$13.71 \pm 2.080 \times \frac{3.55}{\sqrt{22}} \implies 13.71 \pm 1.57$

Resulting Interval: [12.14, 15.28] MPa

Interpretation: We are 95% confident that the true mean load at failure for this alloy is between 12.14 MPa and 15.28 MPa.

Analysis of the Result

The width of a confidence interval is a measure of its precision.

The interval becomes wider (less precise) when:

  • Sample size (n) is smaller.
  • Sample standard deviation (s) is larger.
  • Confidence level ($1-\alpha$) is higher.

To achieve a narrower (more precise) interval, you can:

  • Increase the sample size (n).
  • Decrease the confidence level (e.g., from 99% to 95%).

Quick Comparison: Z-Interval vs. T-Interval

Scenario Distribution Used Formula for Confidence Interval
Population Variance σ² Known Standard Normal (Z) $\bar{x} \pm z_{\alpha/2}\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}$
Population Variance σ² Unknown Student's t $\bar{x} \pm t_{\alpha/2, n-1}\frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}$

Rule of Thumb: For large samples (typically $n \ge 40$), the t-distribution closely approximates the Z-distribution, making the Z-interval a reasonable substitute.

Summary and Conclusion

  • When population variance (σ²) is unknown, use the t-distribution with n-1 degrees of freedom.
  • The confidence interval formula is:
    $\bar{x} \pm t_{\alpha/2, n-1}\frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}$
  • This method provides a reliable range for the true population mean, accounting for the uncertainty of estimating variance from the sample.
  • Key Applications:
    • Measurement Engineering
    • Materials Science
    • Quality Control
